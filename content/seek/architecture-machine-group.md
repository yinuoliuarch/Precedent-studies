## Architecture Machine Group

Founded in 1967 by Nicholas Negroponte and Leon Groisser at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), the Architecture Machine Group (Arch Mac) was an interdisciplinary research laboratory investigating the intersection of architecture, computation, and artificial intelligence. At a time when computers were largely confined to calculation and drafting, the group explored how they could participate in design as intelligent, interactive partners.

Through both theoretical writing and experimental prototypes, the Architecture Machine Group challenged conventional notions of architecture as a fixed object. Instead, it proposed environments that could sense, respond, and adapt through ongoing interaction with their users. These ideas were articulated in Nicholas Negroponte's The Architecture Machine (1970) and Soft Architecture Machines (1975), and tested through projects such as URBAN5, SEEK, and HUNCH. Together, these works laid the foundation for later developments in interactive architecture, responsive environments, and human–computer interaction.

In 1985, the Architecture Machine Group evolved into the MIT Media Laboratory, carrying its interdisciplinary approach into a broader exploration of digital media, computation, design, and emerging technologies.
