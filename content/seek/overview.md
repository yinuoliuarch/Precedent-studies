**SEEK: Architecture as a Responsive Machine**

SEEK was an experimental installation developed by Nicholas Negroponte and the MIT Architecture Machine Group. First exhibited in Software, the landmark exhibition curated by Jack Burnham at the Jewish Museum in New York in 1970, the project investigated how computation, robotics, and living organisms could interact within a shared environment. Rather than presenting architecture as a finished object, SEEK treated it as a dynamic system shaped by continuous exchanges between physical space, machine intelligence, and unpredictable behavior.

How SEEK Worked

The installation consisted of a Plexiglas enclosure filled with hundreds of small cubic blocks that formed a habitat for a colony of gerbils. Suspended above the enclosure, a computer-controlled robotic arm monitored the arrangement of the blocks and reorganized them according to an internal spatial model. As the gerbils moved through the enclosure, they displaced blocks, collapsed structures, and created new configurations. Detecting these changes through sensors, the computer directed the robotic arm to modify the environment, producing an ongoing cycle of observation, interpretation, and physical intervention rather than a predetermined sequence of actions.

Technical System

SEEK was built around a 5 × 8-foot robotic framework with a three-axis moving carriage equipped with an electromagnet, microswitches, and pressure sensors capable of manipulating individual two-inch cubes. An Interdata Model 3 computer maintained an internal representation of the environment, coordinated the robotic arm's movements, and updated its model in response to changes detected within the enclosure.

Architectural Significance

More than a robotic demonstration, SEEK served as a test of the Architecture Machine Group's broader vision for intelligent environments. By replacing human occupants with gerbils whose behavior was spontaneous and impossible to predict, the project examined whether a computational system could interpret and respond to patterns of activity instead of explicit commands. In doing so, SEEK proposed architecture as an ongoing process of adaptation rather than a fixed object, anticipating later developments in responsive environments, interactive architecture, and cybernetic design while challenging conventional notions of authorship, control, and the relationship between designer, user, and machine.
